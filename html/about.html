About us

We are Enerchi

Pythagoras Ecology Works is a California non-profit established specifically to provide a net greenhouse gas reduction through the operation of a zero emissions sawmill works, ecology/STEAM education projects for k-12/college students in target area and useful wood products that benefit the region and community. Every piece of this project is intentional. ​​Each of our activities are directly tied to carbon sequestration, improved education, health and community resources. In 2018, governer Jerry Brown declared a state of emergency in the tree mortality crisis with more than 120 million trees killed by record drought & insects. In order to mitigate further risk of wildfires, the state planned to remove downed dead trees near roadways, utility lines that pose a threat to public safety first. We will be partnering with local public works departments, landowners, businesses, and citizens to remove and mill downed trees before the wood looses its’ quality as lumber. Logs which cannot be milled will be repurposed for particleboard bio-mass wood chip gasification to operate the sawmill plants while reducing our waste and carbon footprint. We will produce wood products that are of service in construction and education (desks, chairs, worktables) which are elegant, portable, and functional. The zero emissions sawmill is designed to produce 2mbf per eight-hour shift. The entire operation is set to use renewable energy sources to power its equipment. The site location will be in Stockton, California and serve the areas between Fresno and Stockton. The project is replicable and scalable. The location we have selected to launch our initiative on is downtown Fresno. As our program expands and grows, we also hope to extend our reach to provide learning spaces for youth in disadvantaged or disaster-affected areas across the United States. The nationwide need for CLT lumber is growing, with capacity built into project design, we will be well positioned to participate as supplier to this market: first by providing mass wood CLT, particle board, plywood, plywood veneer products; harvesting most material from defined emergency zone areas, helping create healthy forest as outlined in CAL Fire priority activity guidelines. Through research, the teaching and learning communities developed will create new products based on testing and applicable uses for fencing, gardening containers, urban raised bed vegetable gardens, covered community spaces, outdoor seating, tables. In addition to our student- focused programming, we are also pursuing $75/ $25 USDA rural utilities infrastructure match grants so we can utilize our nail-free, zero emission, recycled wood designs to develop 5,000+sq.ft. homes for youth who are aging out of foster care. Our Mission

Humble beginnings

Our green solution seeks to save energy and reduce our carbon footprint while creating elegant educational materials for under resourced students so that they can make an investment in themselves and reinvest in others. We believe that every child is deserving of high quality resources and a high quality education. We see personal, environmental, and educational wellbeing as interconnected elements of holistic wellbeing, and seek to restore the ecosystem. Our goals are: Remove and mill downed logs from the roadsides, manufacturing LSL, LVL, CLT, plywood, particleboard, plus specialty lumber. Apply our considerable design knowledge and resources in creation of useful wood products which serve learning needs of school age children, then broader the adult segment. Reforestation, planting of diverse trees throughout the community and cities in collaboration with local and state beautification, watershed creation guideline plans. Design and build vertical & community raised bed gardens deploying hydroponic, aeroponic and aquaponics methodologies utilizing wood milled from emergency zones. Greening of the community. Joseph Loeb is a lifelong entrepreneur, with a vision for how technology is an essential avenue for democratizing opportunity. The year was 1992, during the riots from the death of Latasha Harlins, a 13 year old black girl who was shot after leaving a corner store, that Loeb’s passion for technology and community was seeded. She was the “last straw” in these Rodney King riots of 1992.

As lifelong resident of Los Angeles, Loeb was at the epicenter of the sirens, the smoke, and the anguish that echoed across the nation that spring. Loeb recounts,

“It's amazing how they could understand the humanity in the perpetrators, but not in us. It was a horrible pain.”

Loeb’s response was to take action, to transgress this energy into action. He thought technology was one of the few areas that could “level the playing field”. He got this idea from the old Mission Impossible movie. One of the characters, Greg Morris, a spy disguised as a tech expert, was called into the government office to fix an IT problem. Little did the government workers know that Greg was actually there to help his espionage team infiltrate. Loeb saw how Greg was able to navigate so seamlessly as a black man, simply because he was a tech expert.

This planted seeds for him that 1) technology is a vIable avenue to equalize opportunities in the workforce for black people and 2) technology can be a revolutionary tool to innovate for the betterment of his community.

Since then, Loeb has done work in building computer labs across the country to democratize access to computer software. For his work in education and technology access, he received the Essence Award in technology along with mogul Bill Gates in 2000.
